# Wizard Regulation

**Summary**: The regulatory and governmental reality that GSE clients operate under — not discussed in any GSE source material, but inescapable context for every service line. This page synthesizes a Reddit /r/dndnext thread cataloguing how governments in D&D settings handle powerful spellcasters.

**Sources**: raw/What is the government's relationship with magic and powerful spellcasters in your world?.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-19

## Why this page exists

Every GSE client — [[archibald]], [[delara-venn]], [[halric-bren]], [[iolanthe-marsk]], [[northwatch-academy]] — operates inside a jurisdiction with some posture toward wizards. None of the GSE source material specifies which posture applies. That posture determines everything from whether a sanctum can legally be warded against inspection, to who has standing to investigate an accusation like the cow incident, to whether a diviner's client list is subject to subpoena.

This page does not assert what GSE's specific jurisdictional landscape looks like. It catalogs the patterns visible in community discourse so the gap can be named, and so audience questions implied by it can be tracked.

## The core problem

Several commenters state a version of the same observation: a mid-to-high level caster is a walking strategic threat. Any government worth the name must have *some* answer to that threat, because absent one, the casters *become* the government by default. (source: What is the government's relationship with magic and powerful spellcasters in your world?.md)

> "Any government in a world with 5e's magic users needs to control them. [...] It breaks verisimilitude for me to assume simple feudalism without addressing the walking weapons of mass destruction, any one of which could potentially obliterate a lord's army." — master_of_sockpuppet

> "There is frankly no way whatsoever for governments to limit or control powerful spell casters without those casters doing the enforcing. Which means that they're in charge." — [deleted]

The standard resolution is that either the government *is* a magocracy, or it maintains enough friendly spellcasters to enforce against rogue ones — a balance of magical power.

## Regulatory models

The thread surfaces several distinct approaches. GSE clients could plausibly fall under any of them.

### Guild-based regulation (the Waterdeep model)

Wizards and other casters are required or strongly incentivized to join an enmeshed social group — a guild, order, or mage association. The guild regulates its own members in exchange for privileges and standing. (source: infiltrateoppose)

> "You're a high level wizard? OK — but if you want to live in the city you are going to have to join the Mages and Protectors, or some other relevant guild, show up for your one weekend a month, promise to help out the city if it is attacked, and keep your fellow guild members in line to ensure they are not upsetting the nobles or other guilds."

Implication for GSE: guild membership is both a PR surface and an infosec surface. [[northwatch-academy]] (a guild-adjacent institution) would be both a client and, structurally, a regulator over its own members.

### Registration and licensing

Casters are required to register with the state, sometimes with their foci, staves, or wands individually logged so cast spells can be audited after the fact. (source: FinalDisciple; whalelord09)

> "Staves, wands and focuses had to be registered so you could see the spells that were cast in the past."

Implication for GSE: [[information-security]]'s counter-scrying and warded-comms offerings potentially collide with registration regimes. Warding against state inspection is a service category that GSE source material never explicitly addresses.

### Threat-tier monitoring by level

Multiple commenters describe governments assessing casters as strategic assets at specific thresholds. (source: PanserDragoon)

- **Level 1–4**: low-level magic, anyone can have it, mostly ignored
- **Level 5+**: strategic-asset threshold; watched; local authorities kept informed of movements
- **Level 10+**: city-strategic priority; expected to consult on matters of importance; tracked at all times
- **Level 15+**: nation-leading court wizard or archmage; governments actively court loyalty; hostile defection may be met with assassination
- **Level 20+**: interdimensional, famous across continents; no single government can compel them without an equivalent caster

Implication for GSE: client risk profile scales nonlinearly with level. A mid-level diviner like [[iolanthe-marsk]] has categorically different threat exposure than an archmage retainer client would. GSE's published service mix does not differentiate by client tier.

### Court wizardry and gilded cages

Governments identify magically talented children early, fund their training, and extend a standing offer of position and patronage before the caster's independence hardens. Legal barriers on independent practice raise the price of refusing. (source: Vydsu; Camulius73)

Implication for GSE: the apprentice pipeline ([[sera-oak]]'s infosec apprentice training, the five tavern rules) intersects with state talent acquisition. No GSE source addresses how to advise a client whose apprentice receives a court invitation.

### Enforcement tools

When state counter-magic capacity is real, it tends to look like:

- **Counter-spellers** — dedicated anti-caster squads
- **Magic-eating shackles and nullification gear**
- **Inquisitions** — ideological-theological framing
- **Hit squads** with royal-armory clearance
- **Equivalent casters** — the only genuinely reliable enforcement

(source: CoffeeSorcerer69; iamthesex; Leopath)

Implication for GSE: physical security planning against a state actor is categorically different from planning against a mob or a rival. The [[physical-security]] service page assumes the former threat class; no guidance exists for the latter.

### Cooperation via peer self-regulation (the Dragonlance model)

High-level casters organize into orders that enforce rules on their own members, because the alternative is being put down collectively by the non-magical majority. (source: Transcendentist)

> "They play by their rules. Because if you don't, the three orders lay the law down on you. [...] last time that kind of force was marshaled against them, they lost 3/5 of their strongholds."

Implication for GSE: peer regulation is both a client protection (legitimacy) and a client risk (the order can sanction you). PR for a wizard censured by their own order is a specific problem not covered in the [[crisis-pr-course]].

## What the sources don't tell us about the GSE world

The GSE source material is silent on all of the above. We do not know:

- Whether GSE clients are required to register
- Whether the Sword Coast operates a guild regime
- Whether GSE's services — wards against inspection, counter-scrying, narrative displacement — are legal everywhere they're sold
- How GSE itself is regulated as a firm (as a guild of bards and investigators; as an unlicensed practice)
- Whether client levels factor into GSE's pricing or service mix, and whether high-tier clients trigger state interest by association

These are now tracked as audience questions — see [[questions]].
