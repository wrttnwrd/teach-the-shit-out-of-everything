# Divination Is Not A Security Strategy

_**Physical Security · Rant** · Darkmoon 03_

> **TL;DR** Divination answers questions you thought to ask. It does not answer the question that gets you killed, which is the question you didn't think to ask. Stop relying on it as your primary defense.

Here's the argument I keep having, at dinner parties, with diviners:

"Why would I hire bodyguards? I can _see the future_."

And every time, I have to sit there and explain, patiently, through the appetizers and the main course, why this is not the flex they think it is. I'm going to write it down now so I can just hand people a copy and get on with my soup.

## What divination actually does

Divination answers questions you specifically ask. Good diviners will remind you of this; arrogant ones won't. The spell returns information on the topic you inquired about, with varying precision, limited by how well you framed the question.[^1]

This is useful! It is genuinely useful. If you want to know whether to travel on the east road or the west road, divination is your friend.

What divination does not do:

- Alert you to threats you didn't think to ask about.
- Run continuously in the background while you're asleep.
- Notice a rogue on your balcony right now.
- Replace a human being who is professionally paid to notice rogues on balconies.

## The question problem

Here's the subtlety. The security-relevant question is usually not "am I in danger?" The security-relevant question is usually "is there any threat I haven't considered?"

Divination cannot answer the second question well. By its nature. You are asking the spell to tell you about the edge of your own knowledge, and the spell's output space is bounded by your own ability to specify it.

To put it plainly: the thing that gets a wizard killed is almost never the thing they asked about. It's the thing they would have asked about if only they had known to.[^2]

A good bodyguard, by contrast, is an active pattern-matcher. They do not need you to specify the threat in advance. They see the wrong person in the wrong place and they act. That's the entire point of them.

## The attention problem

Let's say, for argument, that you could fix the question problem. You wake up every morning and run a comprehensive divination about everything that might happen that day, covering every possible threat vector in exhaustive detail.

Fine. You now know about the attack. Do you have the attention to act on the information?

Wizards are introverts. Wizards read. The reason you're thinking about security at all is that you keep losing track of the outside world for three, four, sometimes twelve hours at a stretch because you're deep in a text.[^3] The rogue on the balcony arrives in hour three of the twelve, and your morning divination is already a memory.

Divination gives you knowledge. It does not give you attention. Security is almost entirely an attention problem, not a knowledge problem.

## The timing problem

And finally — even if you solved the question problem and the attention problem — divination operates on the wrong timescale.

Most divinations take minutes to cast. The ones that give genuinely detailed answers take much longer. An assassin's crossbow takes less than a second from decision to bolt. You are not going to cast a spell fast enough to _react_ to divination output while the attack is in progress.

This is why bodyguards stand _in front of you,_ facing outward, already looking at the thing before the thing has done anything. They're a pre-cast spell. They're the counter-cast that lives in the room with you.

## When divination _does_ help with security

I don't want to say it's useless. It isn't. Here's where divination genuinely contributes:

- **Long-range threat assessment.** "Over the next month, will any of my known enemies move against me?" This is a fine question. Act on the answer.
- **Travel planning.** "Is the east road safer than the west road next Tuesday?" Genuinely useful. Bodyguards don't know the roads. Seers do.
- **Post-incident forensics.** After something goes wrong, divination can often tell you what happened in a way no other spell can.
- **Hiring decisions.** "Will this new apprentice betray me within five years?" We wish every wizard would ask this. Most don't.

Used this way, divination is an input into your security posture. It is not the posture itself. The posture is made of humans with swords and wards that trigger on intrusion. Divination tells the humans which roads to watch.

End of rant. If you're a diviner and you disagree, I would genuinely love to argue about this. Bring dinner.

[^1]: The classic failure case: you ask "will I be safe tomorrow" and you are, in fact, safe tomorrow. The assassin arrives the day after. Divination did not lie. You asked the wrong question.

[^2]: I once knew a diviner who asked every morning whether there would be any attempts on his life that day. There never were. He was poisoned by his chef on a Tuesday. "Attempt on my life" had, in his mental model, meant "assassin." The chef was just a friend. The spell answered the question he asked.

[^3]: The ten-hour reading session is the moment of maximum vulnerability. It is also the moment wizards most refuse to give up. I have strong opinions about this.
