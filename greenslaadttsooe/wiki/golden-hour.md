# Golden Hour

**Summary**: The critical window immediately after a crisis becomes public during which the response team's actions disproportionately shape the outcome. Borrowed from PR industry writing, it aligns with and operationalizes GSE's [[narrative-timing]] methodology.

**Sources**: raw/5 Steps for Navigating the First Hour of a Crisis.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-19

## The concept

The Golden Hour is the short stretch of time right after a crisis becomes public during which the response team's actions "directly influence the outcome." (source: 5 Steps for Navigating the First Hour of a Crisis.md)

In the original formulation the window is about sixty minutes. In practice, in a world with fast-moving communication — in a D&D frame, that's sending stones, bards riding ahead of the news, and the tavern telegraph — the window is often much shorter. The underlying principle doesn't change: the first response establishes the frame, and the frame hardens fast.

## Connection to narrative timing

[[narrative-timing]] — [[mirella-thane]]'s core methodology — says that what people hear first becomes the frame. The Golden Hour names the operational corollary: it's the window during which you can still be the one setting the frame instead of reacting to someone else's.

In [[the-cow-incident]], Archibald missed the Golden Hour — he stayed behind the door for eighteen hours. By the time he emerged to explain transmutation theory, the frame was set and every input was interpreted through it ("he has a cow-magic book"). GSE's arrival at hour 27 was effectively a *second* Golden Hour: the shorter window during which the firm could displace the accusation with a true counter-narrative (the goat song) before it set permanently.

## The before-crisis work

The PRSA article's most transferable point: the Golden Hour is won in advance.

- **Choose your team.** Designate leadership and a primary spokesperson *before* a crisis. Auditioning a spokesperson once torches are lit is the wrong time. (source: 5 Steps for Navigating the First Hour of a Crisis.md)
- **Rehearse scenarios.** Walk through likely crisis types so roles and responsibilities are automatic, not improvised.
- **Train people to identify a crisis early.** A shorter detection lag means a longer usable Golden Hour.

This maps directly onto Lesson 09 of the [[crisis-pr-course]] ("Building Reputation Before You Need It") and the good-works program in GSE's [[public-relations]] service: pre-crisis investment multiplies the effectiveness of any in-crisis action.

## The four in-crisis steps

From the PRSA article, lightly adapted for a wizarding audience:

1. **Ensure safety.** If the crisis involves a safety issue — fire, injury, a runaway familiar — handle that first. Contact emergency services or the local authority. Nothing else works if the bodies don't stop piling up. (source: 5 Steps for Navigating the First Hour of a Crisis.md)
2. **Establish a command center.** A central point — physical, or linked via sending stones — where the response team coordinates. The value is not the location but the shared reference point: one version of events, one statement under review, one decision log.
3. **Craft a holding statement.** A short, pre-cleared response that acknowledges the situation without committing to details you don't yet have. Developed with legal counsel (for GSE clients: the wizard's retained counsel or guild liaison) and organizational leadership. Its purpose is to control the narrative while you learn what's actually happening.
4. **Communicate internally first.** Before any public statement, brief apprentices, household staff, and any stakeholders who would feel betrayed to hear it from a bard in the tavern. Internal solidarity before external exposure. This step is easy to skip and expensive to skip.

The fourth step is the most under-practiced. The cow-incident response did not get this right — Archibald's apprentice was the one who called GSE, which suggests no internal crisis protocol existed. See [[the-cow-incident]] and the standing audience question about apprentice crisis-escalation authority in [[questions]].

## Adaptations for wizards

The source is written for corporate PR leaders. The wizarding translations:

- **Spokesperson** = someone other than the wizard. In [[the-cow-incident]], Mirella's first instruction was "go inside." Wizards are bad spokespeople under pressure. An apprentice, a bard retained for the purpose, or a friendly villager is usually better.
- **Command center** = the tower's main hall, or wherever sending-stone traffic converges. For retainer clients, GSE's on-site response team establishes one on arrival.
- **Legal counsel** = the client's guild (if they're in one), their patron, or — in the absence of both — GSE itself. The regulatory landscape matters here; see [[wizard-regulation]].
- **Internal comms** = apprentices, household staff, retained bards, and any bound familiars with enough intellect to talk. People inside the walls hear things and will talk to people outside the walls.
