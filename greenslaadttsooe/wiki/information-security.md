## Threat landscape

GSE identifies four primary information security threats:

### [[scrying]]

The most persistent threat. Counter-scrying sweeps find approximately one active scrying effect per wizard per month. Attackers seek preparation rituals, sleep schedules, and component storage locations. (source: gse-site/information-security.md)

### Social engineering

Manipulated messages from fabricated identities, designed to trick the wizard into casting a specific spell, revealing information, or sending valuable components. A particularly sophisticated variant involves tricking the wizard into completing a magical triangulation that enables scrying. (source: gse-site/information-security.md, gse-site/post-scam.md)

### Supply-chain attacks

Bribing a component supplier's apprentice to introduce sympathetic links (e.g., into diamond dust batches) that connect back to a scrying pool. GSE inspects and can replace client supply chains. (source: gse-site/information-security.md)

### Insider threats

Almost always the apprentice, almost never malicious — typically information shared inadvertently at social events. GSE conducts background checks, trust audits, and "lifestyle monitoring" (with staff awareness). (source: gse-site/information-security.md)

## The three-con taxonomy

GSE categorizes ~90% of scams into three types:

1. **Fake nobility** — An obscure duke/baron needs a wizard's help; requests a "gesture of good faith" (valuable components) before the promised reward. Exploits wizards' desire for recognition.
2. **Fake emergency** — The wizard's apprentice or associate is allegedly injured/arrested/taken; requires immediate, irreversible action. Exploits urgency and emotional bonds.
3. **Fake admirer** — A scholar or pilgrim flatters the wizard's work with suspiciously specific technical praise. Goal: spellbook access, scrying focus placement, or long-term information extraction.

**Meta-rule**: "If a stranger is asking you to make an irreversible decision on an urgent timeline, the answer is no. Every time. Without exception." (source: gse-site/post-scam.md)

## Apprentice security

Apprentices are the most common security vulnerability — not through malice but through normal social behavior. GSE's "What To Say At The Tavern" training covers five rules:

1. Never name the location of a component or book.
2. Never confirm current research topics.
3. Never confirm the master's schedule.
4. If someone asks strangely specific questions, leave.
5. Report every suspicious conversation to the master the next day.

The complementary obligation on the master: receive every report calmly and say "thank you for telling me." Punishing false alarms trains apprentices to stop reporting. (source: gse-site/post-apprentice.md)

## Standard engagement model

1. **Inbound sweep** — One day. Find and cut active threats.
2. **Process audit** — Trace messages inbound and information outbound. Find leaks.
3. **Standing retainer** — Ongoing monitoring, monthly reports, quarterly apprentice refreshers.

(source: gse-site/information-security.md)
