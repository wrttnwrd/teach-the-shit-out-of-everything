# Call Transcript — 005
**Date:** Darkmoon 28
**Type:** Prospect · Initial discovery call
**Caller:** Master Tenric Vosh, Dean of Admissions, Northwatch Academy of Evocation
**GSE rep:** Mirella Thane, Head of Public Relations
**Duration:** 34 minutes
**Recorded with consent:** Yes
**Status:** Qualified — PR, but organizational engagement (not individual wizard), requires follow-up

---

**MIRELLA:** Dean Vosh, thanks for reaching out. I'll be honest, I was curious when I saw the academy's name on the intake. We don't get many institutional inquiries.

**TENRIC:** No, I would imagine not. You mostly work with individuals.

**MIRELLA:** Almost exclusively. So this will be a learning conversation for both of us. What's the situation?

**TENRIC:** Three of our third-year students were involved in an incident off-campus last month. At a tavern in Northwatch proper. A brawl started, they intervened — they say defensively — and the brawl ended with significant property damage and one broken arm. Ours was not the broken arm.

**MIRELLA:** Mm.

**TENRIC:** The town watch interviewed the students, released them. No charges. The tavern owner was compensated. By all official measures, the matter is closed.

**MIRELLA:** But.

**TENRIC:** But the Northwatch Courier ran a column the next week with the headline "When Is A School Not A Menace?" The piece was unsigned. It cited the tavern incident and three other events — all minor, all going back more than two years. There has been a town-council petition. We have received four withdrawal letters from parents of current students.

**MIRELLA:** How many current students?

**TENRIC:** Forty-one.

**MIRELLA:** Four withdrawals out of forty-one is significant.

**TENRIC:** We are not in a strong position to absorb it.

**MIRELLA:** Understood. What outcome are you looking for?

**TENRIC:** Ideally, the Courier stops running pieces. The petition goes away. We get through next admissions cycle without further loss. Longer term, I'd like the academy to have — (pause) — a more constructive presence in the town.

**MIRELLA:** Those are three different timelines and three different projects. Let me be candid: we can probably help with the longer-term work. We can probably help with the admissions-cycle framing. I'm less sure about the Courier piece and the petition specifically, because we don't have much experience working inside a single town against a single publication. Most of our work is dispersed across village networks.

**TENRIC:** You're telling me you might not be the right firm.

**MIRELLA:** I'm telling you I want to be honest about where our experience is strongest and where it isn't.

**TENRIC:** I appreciate that.

**MIRELLA:** _[⚑ Gap question]_ Let me ask you something, because it'll affect my answer. What's the academy's relationship to the Mage's Guild? Are you affiliated, independent, chartered by someone else?

**TENRIC:** We're chartered by the Guild but operate independently. Financially, academically, and in terms of our disciplinary process. The Guild would prefer not to be involved in something like this.

**MIRELLA:** (pause) That answers part of it. I'll tell you what I know and where I'm uncertain. Individual wizards — we handle dozens a year. Reputation problems between a wizard and a village — that's our bread and butter. An academy, which is a collective entity with its own charter, its own external affiliations, students who are in some sense minors, and a publication as an adversary — there are parts of this I know how to handle and parts where I'd be working outside my normal range.

**TENRIC:** Can you be more specific?

**MIRELLA:** The parts I know: framing the academy's public narrative, training your students and faculty in what to say in town, building the good-works program that changes the base rate of tolerance. Those translate directly from our individual-wizard practice.

**TENRIC:** And the parts you don't?

**MIRELLA:** Two things. First, the Courier specifically. I don't know the editor, I don't know what motivates the column, and I don't have existing relationships in Northwatch's press community the way I have them in the village network. That's learnable, but it's a cost. Second, institutional politics. The Guild's preferences, your board, your faculty's comfort with the kind of work we'd do — those are variables I'm not used to managing. Most of my clients make their own decisions. Committees are a different beast.

**TENRIC:** Those are fair cautions.

**MIRELLA:** I'd rather surface them now than six weeks in. Here's what I'd propose: a two-week paid assessment. I come to Northwatch, I talk to your dean's office, I read the Courier's archives, I walk the town, I meet the tavern owner, I listen. At the end I give you a written brief with three things: what the actual problem is, what I think we can do about it, and — most importantly — whether I think we should be the firm doing it. If the answer to the third is no, the brief will include recommendations for who should.

**TENRIC:** And the cost of the assessment?

**MIRELLA:** Four hundred gold, flat. Plus travel. I'll send you a statement of work tomorrow.

**TENRIC:** That seems reasonable for what you're describing. Let me take it to the dean.

**MIRELLA:** One last thing. If you proceed, I'd want to do the assessment myself. I don't want to hand this to a junior. This is too outside our normal pattern.

**TENRIC:** Noted. And appreciated. Thank you, Ms. Thane.

**MIRELLA:** Send me a reply by Rainmoon 05 either way. I'll hold the time.
