# If A Duke You've Never Met Needs Mithril, It's A Scam

_**Information Security** · Darkmoon 19_

> **TL;DR** Almost every scam we see is one of three cons: fake nobility, fake emergency, fake admirer. Learn these three and you'll spot nine out of ten before they get anywhere.

Here's the thing about scams: they're not creative. They feel creative when you're inside one, because your brain is telling you an exciting story about mithril and dukes. From the outside, they're boring. The same three cons, rotated slightly, running since the invention of the sending stone.

I've wanted to write this post for a long time. If a single wizard reads it and keeps their mithril, I consider it a success.

## Con one: fake nobility

The message arrives with an impressive seal. The writer is a duke, a baron, a marcher lord — someone with title enough to impress but obscure enough not to recognize. They have a problem only a wizard can solve. They will pay handsomely. They need a small gesture of good faith first: a vial of this, a pouch of that, a short-term loan of a rare component.[^1]

**Why it works:** wizards like to feel important, and they like to be recognized by the nobility, and they particularly like to be recognized by _obscure_ nobility because it feels like a secret. The con exploits all three.

**How to spot it:**

- The title is real-sounding but unverifiable without a week's ride.
- The request involves sending something valuable _before_ the promised reward arrives.
- There's a deadline. There's always a deadline.

**What to do:** nothing. Literally nothing. Don't reply. If the duke is real, he'll send again. If he's not, he won't. The only cost of waiting is the scammer's time, which is fine.

## Con two: fake emergency

Your apprentice — who is away at the academy, or visiting relatives, or traveling for research — has been injured. Or arrested. Or taken. The message arrives by fast courier, or sending stone, or, in the more sophisticated version, by an actual tearful person at your door.

They need money. Or a rare component. Or, in the really audacious versions, a specific spell cast at a specific location, right now.[^2]

**Why it works:** emergency hijacks the part of your brain that checks for scams. Also, if it's your apprentice, you _care,_ which is exactly the lever.

**How to spot it:**

- The situation requires an immediate, irreversible action on your part.
- The details are just vague enough that you can't verify them without acting first.
- You are asked not to contact anyone else — "it would make things worse."

**What to do:** stop. Breathe. Contact the apprentice directly through a channel they control. If you can't, contact someone who knows them — their academy tutor, their family. Five minutes of verification has never lost anyone an apprentice. Five minutes of _not_ verifying has lost several towers.

## Con three: fake admirer

This one is the most embarrassing, which is why it works. Someone — a young scholar, a pilgrim, a traveling sage — has read your work and wishes to study with you. They've come a long way. They want nothing but a few weeks at your side, and perhaps a small demonstration of your most interesting current research.

Some version of this con ends with the scammer walking out of the tower with a copy of your spellbook. Some version ends with them planting a scrying focus in your sanctum. The elegant version ends with them marrying into your staff and spending five years gently extracting every secret you have.

**Why it works:** wizards are lonely, and wizards are vain, and the con exploits both at once. A stranger arrives who has read _your_ monograph, and praises the _one_ footnote nobody else noticed, and suddenly you are having the conversation you've wanted to have for years.[^3]

**How to spot it:**

- The flattery is specific and technical and perfectly pitched to your ego.
- They have no institutional affiliation you can verify, or the affiliation is with an institution that burned down recently.
- They arrive _alone._ Real scholars travel with baggage, colleagues, letters of introduction. Admirers travel light because they came from a tavern down the road.

**What to do:** let them stay in the village, not the tower. Any real scholar will accept this. No scammer will — there's no spellbook in the village.

## The meta-rule

Here's the underlying principle, the one rule that makes all the specific tells redundant:

> If a stranger is asking you to make an irreversible decision on an urgent timeline, the answer is no. Every time. Without exception.

Engrave that on something. Put it over your sending stone. A real opportunity will still be a real opportunity in the morning. A real friend in need will accept that you want to verify. A real duke will send again.

[^1]: The gesture of good faith is always valuable and always non-refundable. That's how you know.

[^2]: We had one case where the scammer talked a wizard into casting _sending_ to a location the scammer specified, which completed a magical triangulation that let them scry the wizard's sanctum. Beautiful con, in a horrible way.

[^3]: The footnote is the giveaway, actually. Real admirers rarely make it to your footnotes. They got bored. This is how you know they're faking.
