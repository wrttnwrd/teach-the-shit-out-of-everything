# The Cow Incident: A Case Study In How Not To Respond

_**Public Relations** · Frostmoon 02_

> **TL;DR** A wizard was accused of transforming a village child into a cow. He hadn't. His first three instincts were all wrong. The fourth instinct — calling us — saved the tower. Here's what to do differently.

The wizard in this story has given us permission to publish it, on condition of anonymity. We'll call him Archibald, because that isn't his name and he would hate being called Archibald.[^1]

What follows is a straight recounting of the incident, hour by hour, from the moment the accusation was first made to the moment the tower was safe again. The wizard made four decisions. Three were bad. One was good. Here they are in order.

## Hour 0: The accusation

A farmer's daughter, age eleven, had gone missing for most of an afternoon. She turned up at supper. She was fine. A cow had also been seen near Archibald's tower that morning — a cow that nobody in the village recognized.

By dusk, the miller's wife had connected these two facts. By full dark, the connection was the only story in the tavern. By morning, it was fact.

### Bad decision one: refusing to engage

Archibald's first response, when a concerned neighbor came to the tower door, was to refuse to open it. His logic was that the accusation was absurd and therefore did not merit a response.

This is exactly backwards. **The more absurd the accusation, the more important it is to respond.** Absurd accusations are the ones that grow. Reasonable accusations burn themselves out. Ridiculous accusations get set to music.[^2]

## Hour 18: Escalation

By the next afternoon, the accusation had moved from the tavern to the village green. People were gathering. Not yet a mob — mobs don't form that fast — but a _concerned assembly,_ which is what mobs are before they're mobs.

### Bad decision two: the explanation

Archibald, finally rattled, came down to the village green and attempted to explain. He explained:

- The schools of magic, and which school transmutation belongs to.
- The material component cost of polymorph effects.
- The difference between a true transformation and an illusion.
- The fact that he, personally, had not cast a transmutation spell in some weeks, and could produce his component logbook to prove it.

I want you to picture, for a moment, what it looks like to a group of frightened villagers when a wizard shows up and offers to produce his _component logbook_.

They heard: "I have a book. In the book, I record every time I do cow magic." They heard it very clearly. You cannot unhear a thing like that.

## Hour 26: Peak crisis

The assembly became a mob. Torches were produced. A blacksmith's hammer got involved, which is always a sign things have progressed beyond conversation.

### Bad decision three: the light show

Archibald, in a final attempt to assert his peaceful intent, cast a harmless light spell — a simple _dancing lights_ — to illuminate the courtyard. He intended it as a gesture of openness. A demonstration that he had nothing to hide.

The villagers saw: a wizard casting a spell at them.

The situation got much worse immediately.

## Hour 27: Our arrival

Archibald's apprentice, who had more sense than her master, sent to us at hour 24. Our response team arrived at hour 27, which is about as fast as we can do it without teleporting, and we would have teleported but Archibald's wards would have killed us.[^3]

### Good decision one: letting us run it

This is the decision that saved the tower. When our lead — [Mirella Thane](bio-mirella-thane.md), who heads our PR practice — arrived, she said three things to Archibald, in order:

1. "Stop talking."
2. "Go inside."
3. "Do not come out until I tell you to."

He did all three. That is the only reason this is a case study and not an obituary.

## The response

Here is what we did, in the order we did it:

**First, we produced the child.** Our team had, on the ride in, already confirmed through a local contact that the girl was home and safe. We asked her family if she would be willing to stand with us in the courtyard for ten minutes. They agreed. She came. She was visibly, unmistakably, not a cow.

**Second, we acknowledged the mistake.** Not Archibald's mistake. _Ours._ Our lead said, publicly, "We understand how this looked. We'd have been worried too." This cost nothing and it took ninety percent of the heat out of the crowd.[^4]

**Third, we paid.** We paid the miller for his troubles. We paid the blacksmith for the hammer — he hadn't lost a hammer, we just paid him for the hammer. The hammer is a ritual. It doesn't have to be expensive. The gesture is what matters.

**Fourth, we changed the story.** We had a bard in the tavern the next night with a new song about a time Archibald had healed a sick goat. (He had. We'd checked his logs.) The new song was in the air before the old song had finished traveling.

## What it cost

Total spend: roughly six silver pieces in reparations, our standard crisis retainer, and four nights of bard time. A good week of Archibald's research, lost to the fallout.

Total cost of _not_ calling us, based on what would have happened in the next two hours: one tower. One library. One wizard.

We have this conversation a lot. The math is always this stupid.
