# Brief: When Angry Villagers Come For Your Tower

**Answers questions from questions.md:** Q38 (angry villagers — wards or PR?). Case study exists as a post-mortem, but no standalone piece teaches the incipient-crisis version — the hour when a wizard still thinks this is a perimeter problem.

**Size / type:** Teach-small · Upgrade. The content lives in `post-cow.md` and Lessons 02–04 of the [[crisis-pr-course]], but only as a narrative. This brief reframes it as a diagnosis-and-response piece for the wizard who is *in* the first hour, not reading about someone else's.

## Reader

A wizard whose village is turning — not a mob yet, but the temperature is wrong. Farmers are standoffish at market. The innkeeper stopped waving. An apprentice reports a verse about you in the tavern that wasn't there last week. The wizard's instinct is to reach for wards, bodyguards, or a well-rehearsed explanation. None of those are the answer.

## Concrete problem manifestations

- A child went missing for an afternoon and you hear your name in the story when she turns up fine.
- A neighbor knocks. You don't open the door. An hour later there are three of them.
- A bailiff's messenger arrives. You have no prepared reply, so you give a long one.
- You are considering casting something visible — a light, a ward, a flourish — to "show you mean no harm."
- There is a song. There is always a song eventually, and yours now has a verse.

## Brand-agnostic teaching (biggest part)

**Step one: diagnose the problem. It is almost certainly PR.**

- Mob events, not magical attacks, account for the overwhelming majority of tower losses. If the people at your gate are ordinary villagers, wards are the wrong budget line.
- Magical responses escalate because they confirm the accusation's emotional premise — that you are something the village cannot read. A spell cast in public during a reputation crisis is a spell cast *at* the people watching, regardless of intent.

**Step two: stop doing the three things your instincts want.**

1. **Silence.** Silence does not let absurd accusations burn out. It lets them set to music. Respond fast, even if only to acknowledge that you have heard the concern.
2. **Explanation.** Do not describe the schools of magic. Do not offer to produce your component logbook. The listener hears "I have a book in which I record every time I do cow magic." Technical correctness is not the unit villagers are working in.
3. **Demonstration.** Do not cast anything in front of a scared crowd. Not dancing lights. Not a minor illusion. Nothing. A wizard casting a spell at a mob looks like a wizard casting a spell at a mob.

**Step three: run the response in this order.**

1. **Produce evidence, not arguments.** If someone thinks you turned a child into a cow, the response is the child, standing in the courtyard, visibly not a cow. Every time. Skip the theory.
2. **Acknowledge the fear, not the accusation.** These are two different statements. "We understand how this looked. We'd have been worried too" costs nothing and removes most of the heat. Arguing the accusation keeps the accusation in the air.
3. **Reparation as ritual.** Pay the miller for his troubles. Pay the blacksmith for a hammer he didn't lose. This is symbolic, not an admission of fault — a public gesture that the village's grievance is something you take seriously enough to put silver against.
4. **Displace the narrative with a true competing one.** You do not erase the first story; you give the tavern a better one, and you do it before the first story finishes traveling. It must be true — a real thing you actually did. Spin based on falsehood always collapses.

**The unglamorous precondition.** None of the above works if you meet the village for the first time on the day of the crisis. The pre-crisis version of this piece — the good-works program, the quarterly reputation audit, the village relationships built before they are needed — is the higher-leverage version. If you are reading this in a panic, do the above, and the week after it is over, start that work.

## What to avoid

- Telling the cow-incident story beat-by-beat. This is not the case study — it is the operating manual a reader applies to their own courtyard. Reference the cow incident; do not retell it.
- Any sentence that reads as spin advice. The "no lying" contract language is load-bearing for GSE's credibility and for the piece's.
- Implying the response works if the wizard is the one delivering it. Archibald going inside and shutting up was the load-bearing move. If the reader is the kind of wizard this brief is written for, the spokesperson is not them.
- Compressing the response steps into a four-word listicle. Each step has a failure mode that matters more than the label.
- Treating this as a magical problem with a PR footnote. The framing is the teaching — PR is the primary defense, and wards are downstream of it.
