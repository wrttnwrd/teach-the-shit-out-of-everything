# TTSOOE: A Primer

Context for when I ask you to help with teaching content — brainstorming topics, surfacing customer questions from the wiki, drafting briefs, evaluating existing content.

## Core idea

Algorithms (organic search, AI results, ChatGPT, LinkedIn, YouTube) sell trust. No trust, no business. Teaching builds trust. So algorithms reward teaching, and so do customers. Teaching drives the signals that prove trustworthiness: likes, links, citations, shares, dwell time.

That's the whole strategy. Teach real things to real people; the marketing works.

## What counts as teaching

Real learning from real experts, frictionless, high return on time invested. "Frictionless" is load-bearing: no gates, no forms mid-lesson, no hiding the ball. A CTA at the end is fine; one that interrupts the lesson defeats the mechanism.

It's marketing teaching, not pure education, so it does three things in sequence:

1. **The problem** — prove you understand the reader's situation by naming the concrete, Tuesday-afternoon version. Not "businesses struggle with X."
2. **The brand-agnostic solution** — how the reader fixes this regardless of what they buy. The biggest part. If it shrinks to funnel faster, it isn't TTSOOE.
3. **Why your solution is best** — only after 1 and 2. Integrated or trailing. Light touch.

Skip 2 and it's lead-gen bait. Skip 3 and there's no marketing payoff.

## Two sizes

- **Teach big, hit big.** Major efforts (Michelin Guide, Linode's tutorial library). Budget, time, ROI expectations. Brand-defining when they land; career-ending when they don't. It's OK to offer some of these, but only if you also include small ideas. And always caution the reader about big ideas.

- **Teach small, miss small.** Examples include: Blog posts, LinkedIn posts, social posts on networks like Instagram, little snippets of information on existing pages, really short phone-recorded video, sharing others' smart ideas with context. These are small, compact messages that don't require a big investment, and may not require a click by the user. Everyday mode, low risk, compounding. Make sure you always include these.

## Where real questions come from

Sources roughly by signal quality:

- **Support call transcripts** — highest signal, real people in their own words.
- **Sales calls** — objections and confusions are teaching opportunities in disguise.
- **Reddit / community threads** — unfiltered.
- **Direct conversations** — underrated.
- **PAA / AlsoAsked** — real behavior, sanitized by the search box.
- **Competitor content** — finds bad coverage (upgrades) and gaps (new stuff).
- **Client's own site** — contradictions, gaps, things to upgrade.
- **Existing answers that suck** — upgrade opportunities with built-in demand.

The wiki holds interpreted results of this raw material. The `/raw` folder contains the originals. Your job is usually to pattern-match across it and surface what's worth teaching.

## What good looks like

- Reader could solve their problem using only this piece, without buying anything.
- Nothing in it would make the author cringe to share.
- Doesn't hide the ball.
- Addresses concrete problem manifestations, not abstractions.
- Brand-agnostic teaching is the biggest part.

## What to avoid

- Keyword-quota filler.
- Hide-the-ball teases that gate the value.
- Saccharine corporate tone.
- Teaching what the client doesn't actually know — readers smell it.
- "10 tips" listicles that compress teaching into nothing.
- Length mismatched to topic (thin 101s on complex topics; padded posts on trivial ones).

## Tone

Plain, direct, no marketing-speak. Mild profanity fine when it fits. Real person who knows the topic talking to another real person who wants to learn it. Content that reads like marketing produces content that reads like marketing, which breaks the trust mechanism.

## Common requests

- **"What questions are customers asking?"** — pattern-match the wiki, cluster, rank by frequency and whether a good answer already exists.
- **"What should I write about?"** — topic ideation from the wiki. Classify each as 101 / upgrade / new stuff and flag teach-small vs. teach-big.
- **"Write a brief for X."** — cover: reader, concrete problem manifestations, brand-agnostic teaching skeleton (biggest part), where the client fits, sources, what to avoid for this topic.
- **"Is this piece any good?"** — evaluate against the gut-checks above. Name specific failures.
- **"Upgrade this competitor's piece."** — identify what's unclear/incomplete/inaccurate, sketch the better version.

Ground output in the wiki when relevant material exists. Don't invent customer language when real customer language is sitting right there.

## Push back if

- Pure keyword play with no real teaching opportunity.
- Reader too vague ("small business owners," "marketers").
- Client has no legitimate expertise in the topic.
- Length conflicts with the bucket.
- Obviously quota-filling.

Flag the problem rather than produce a confident brief for a piece that shouldn't exist.

## Details

1. Any content briefs should include at least one frictionless call to action. "Frictionless" means very little effort on the part of the user and no gating of content.
2. Any content briefs should include one or more experts the writer can talk to
